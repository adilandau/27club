Early Life

The Delta

Robert Leroy Johnson was born on May 8, 1911, in Hazlehurst, Mississippi, into the world of the Deep South Delta. He was the eleventh child of Julia Major Dodds and one of the most mythologized figures in American music history. Raised in hardship across the Mississippi Delta, Johnson drifted between farmwork and music from a young age.

As a teenager, Johnson became obsessed with playing the blues, spending long nights listening to established bluesmen like Son House and Willie Brown. His early attempts were met with ridicule — he was known for being so bad that musicians would hide when he took out his guitar.

Legacy

The Crossroads Myth

The legend surrounding Robert Johnson is inseparable from the music. According to folklore, Johnson traveled to a crossroads at midnight — possibly the intersection of Highways 61 and 49 in Clarksdale, Mississippi — and sold his soul to the devil in exchange for mastery of the guitar. When he returned, his playing was so extraordinarily changed that his contemporaries could only explain it through the supernatural.

His influence on rock and roll is immeasurable. Eric Clapton called him "the most important blues singer that ever lived." Led Zeppelin, Keith Richards, Bob Dylan, and countless others have cited Johnson as a foundational inspiration. He is the beginning of the thread that connects Delta blues to rock music as we know it.

Music Career

Twenty-Nine Songs

Robert Johnson recorded only twenty-nine songs over two sessions — in 1936 in San Antonio and in 1937 in Dallas — yet those recordings represent some of the most important documents in American musical history. His guitar technique was ahead of its time, deploying bass lines, treble lead, and rhythm simultaneously, in a way that suggested multiple musicians playing at once.

His vocals carried an eerie, urgent quality — an intimacy with darkness that made listeners believe every word. Songs like "Hellhound on My Trail" and "Me and the Devil Blues" seemed less like compositions and more like testimony. Johnson died at 27, poisoned — possibly by a jealous husband whose wife he had been seeing. The exact circumstances have never been confirmed.
