Early Life

Cheltenham

Lewis Brian Hopkin Jones was born on February 28, 1942, in Cheltenham, England, to a middle-class family. A precocious child, he was playing the piano by age six and the guitar by his teens. Despite academic promise, he was drawn irresistibly to jazz and R&B, against the wishes of his conservative parents.

By his early twenties, Jones had fathered multiple children with different women and was living a chaotic life, drifting between odd jobs and obsessive music practice. He moved to London with a singular ambition: to form a blues band and bring American R&B to British audiences.

Legacy

The Stone Who Founded the Stones

Brian Jones founded The Rolling Stones in 1962, placed the advertisement that recruited Mick Jagger and Keith Richards, and named the band. For years he was the group's unquestioned leader — the one who arranged their sound, directed their style, and pushed them toward an eclectic, adventurous musical identity.

He is remembered as one of rock's most gifted multi-instrumentalists — capable of mastering virtually any instrument he touched. As the band grew around him, his influence waned alongside his mental health. He was fired from the group just weeks before his death. Found drowned in his swimming pool on July 3, 1969 — the first of the 27 Club's rock era.

Music Career

Multi-Instrumentalist

Jones's musical genius was his versatility. He played guitar, harmonica, sitar, dulcimer, recorder, piano, and organ — often adding unexpected instruments to Rolling Stones recordings that defined their psychedelic era. His sitar work on "Paint It Black" and his dulcimer on "Lady Jane" remain iconic contributions to British rock history.

By 1967 and 1968, drug arrests, legal troubles, and the deterioration of his relationships within the band had pushed Jones to the margins. He was physically and mentally broken. His contributions to later albums were minimal. On June 8, 1969, he was officially dismissed from the band he had created. He was dead within a month.
