Early Life

North London

Amy Jade Winehouse was born on September 14, 1983, in Southgate, north London, to Mitchell Winehouse, a taxi driver, and Janis Winehouse, a pharmacist. She grew up in a Jewish household with a deep love of jazz — her grandmother Cynthia dated the saxophonist Ronnie Scott, and her father would sing Frank Sinatra songs around the house. By thirteen, Amy was writing her own songs.

She attended the BRIT School for Performing Arts in Croydon, though she spent most of her time there skipping classes. Her voice was already fully remarkable. At sixteen she began performing with the National Youth Jazz Orchestra. At seventeen she sent a demo to a manager and was signed to a label within a year.

Legacy

Back to Black

Amy Winehouse was the most important British female vocalist of her generation — a voice of extraordinary depth, control, and personality, rooted in jazz and soul but utterly contemporary. Her sophomore album Back to Black (2006) won five Grammy Awards, tying the record for most won by a female artist in a single night. It has sold over twenty million copies worldwide.

She died on July 23, 2011, in her Camden home from accidental alcohol poisoning. Her blood alcohol level was more than five times the UK drink-drive limit. She was twenty-seven. The mourning was global. Camden became a shrine. Her death felt both entirely predictable and completely shocking — which is the cruelest kind.

Music Career

The Voice

Amy's debut album Frank (2003) arrived when she was nineteen — a precocious, jazz-inflected collection that announced a significant new talent. Critics noted her extraordinary vocal control, her songwriting wit, and the emotional directness she brought to material that lesser artists would have softened. But it was nothing compared to what came next.

Back to Black (2006), produced with Mark Ronson, fused the sound of 1960s girl groups, soul, and jazz with searingly personal songwriting. "Rehab," "Tears Dry on Their Own," "Love Is a Losing Game" — each song was a complete world. The album remains one of the defining British records of the 2000s. Her final public performance, visibly troubled, was in June 2011. She was dead six weeks later.
